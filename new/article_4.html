LIBERATION IS AN INSIDE JOB

by Khetsile Dlamini

LIBERATION IS AN INSIDE JOB

31/07/2021

If there are two things I have learnt from teaching History and observing democratic movements, aside from that often the more things change, the more they say the same is:

1. There’s always a group of people that feel left out or not properly represented, the minorities and
2. When it’s time to deliver, whoever wins repeats the cycle of lining their own pockets and forgets about the people on the ground.

This explains why there would be people who are seemingly ‘on the fence’. Can I just mention that this is in no way an attack on any political group or association. As a black homophile woman, of course I want a democratic society where I can decide who I want to trust with my vote as easily as who I want to spend my life with, whether they pee standing or sitting. But both seem farfetched, don’t they? The good thing about being Swazi is that I never have to worry about the colour of my skin, and if I did, well, we always have glutathione.

Now, being a Dlamini also means I could, by some stroke of luck, wake up head of government – only I am a woman and Hospital Hill is no place for women – strike one. Unless of course, we were to have a democratically elected prime minister. So, snapping out of my political ambitions means waking up to the reality that I could never get medical insurance for my partner of eight years with graves disease because well, what is the nature of our relationship? – strike two.

In essence what does this mean? You guessed it – I AM A MINORITY. And no, I am not on the fence – what I am is WONDERING. I am wondering what democracy means for minorities such as myself. Equality…50% of women elected to parliament instead of the current flimsy 30% stipulated by the Constitution, basic human rights and recognition of the LGBTIQ? Liberation from what? Discrimination, I hope, because if democracy means ushering in a new system of government where we still have to fight for acceptance; an ESwatini where we still have to liberate people from hate, judgement, discrimination, chauvinism and inequality, then, can we all, on cue borrow from Msholozi’s “Yiiiiiiinde lendlela esiy’hambayo – yiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiinde…”

Liberation starts with you from within. You must be ready to give what you expect to receive. To receive freedom from any external form of oppression, first free yourself from mental slavery, unnecessary hate and so-called phobias.
