Phelele Fakudze writes for us, another Swazi In Diaspora

by Phelele Fakudze

The Diaspora - At what cost?

24/07/2021

I left Eswatini at 18 years old, shortly after being denied a Government scholarship to study what I wanted to study, and had to get into fast-gear mode of looking for foreign aid to get me my education. I am 38 years old today and still live and work outside Eswatini.

It has become easy, almost default, for the young LiSwati to aspire to leave the country. It looks like a glorious thing to be able to go to school in country X or to work for international company Y. And it would be, if it was free will and choice. But fundamentally, this longing to leave, comes from a deep-rooted place of escaping nothingness. Finding yourself in an environment that is smaller than your dreams. Living in a garden that appears to be flourishing, but somehow does not nurture you. Breaking your back in labor, for a country that does not serve your basic needs as a citizen.

Many Swazis in diaspora today left Eswatini for this reason. For the ordinary LiSwati, there is a level at which there is not much further that your skills can take you. There is not much louder that your voice can get. Either from your social positioning, your last name or your gender. Does this not sound ridiculous? How did we create a country where leaving it is an accomplishment?

The reality is we feel displaced. We do not have any “permanency” in life. Our lives lack a certain level of stability and guaranteed continuity. We live in 2-year, 3-year, 5-year batches of scholarships, of contracts, of permits, of visas. We hold Swazi passports, but in foreign countries. Where do we really belong? Our voices seem to have diminished over the years too, because why should we polished statues of a country that did not invest in us? Why should we be expected to perform patriotism? In our home country, we are just a Passport Number. In our host country, we are another piece of paper. So where do we really belong? Where do our cries land?

And yet ironically, even EmaSwati living in Eswatini can also relate to feeling displaced. We cannot get basic, efficient healthcare. We cannot get support to study. We cannot get the jobs we want. We cannot be promoted to the positions we deserve. We cannot start successful companies. We cannot diversify our art. We can not raise girls safely. So where are we?

Collectively, EmaSwati living in and out of the country are tired. But if we can unite our tired voices, we are louder. We have power. It is our country, and we cannot continue to fulfill a system where children work hard so they can go to America. No. We want to see a country that has adequate and equal opportunities for EVERYONE. A country whose education system nurtures ALL talents. Institutions that serve the people. Opportunity for growth, hope and motivation. If not for us now, for our children and theirs. Our dream is to see an Eswatini where leaving the country should be by choice, not by force.
